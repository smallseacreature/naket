BBMD — BACnet Broadcast Management Device
A BACnet device that forwards BACnet broadcast traffic between IP subnets. Normal IP routers do not forward broadcasts, so BBMDs provide that functionality.

BDT — Broadcast Distribution Table
A list stored by a BBMD containing other BBMDs or subnet destinations that should receive forwarded broadcasts.

A BDT entry is one row in a BBMD’s Broadcast Distribution Table.
“Here is another BACnet/IP subnet or BBMD that should receive forwarded BACnet broadcasts.”

A single entry is 10 bytes:

4 bytes   BBMD IPv4 address
2 bytes   BACnet UDP port
4 bytes   broadcast distribution mask

Example conceptually:

IP address:  192.168.60.1
UDP port:    47808
Mask:        255.255.255.0

Each row tells the BBMD:

“Here is another BACnet/IP subnet or BBMD that should receive forwarded BACnet broadcasts.”

Normal IP routers usually do not forward broadcast packets between subnets.

So, when a BACnet device sends a broadcast such as Who-Is, its local BBMD checks its BDT and forwards that broadcast toward the BBMDs listed in the table.

A BBMD is a role, usually enabled on one controller, router, server, or dedicated device for an IP subnet. 
Ordinary BACnet devices simply send and receive BACnet traffic; they do not maintain a BDT unless they are acting as a BBMD.

A BBMD does not need to be a separate physical box. The BBMD functionality may run inside an existing building controller or workstation

A BDT is security-sensitive because it is essentially BACnet broadcast routing policy. Whoever can change it can influence where BACnet discovery and broadcast traffic travels.

The main concerns are:

Unauthorized BDT modification: 
A Write-BDT message could add an attacker-controlled destination, remove legitimate BBMDs, redirect broadcasts, 
or expand BACnet traffic into an unintended subnet. 
Treat function 0x01 as a configuration-changing operation and alert on it unless it occurs during approved maintenance.

Topology disclosure: 
Read-BDT and Read-BDT-ACK reveal the IP addresses, ports, and masks of BBMD peers. 
That gives an observer useful information about the BACnet network’s structure.
Broadcast amplification or storms: Incorrect, circular, or malicious BDT entries can cause excessive forwarded broadcasts, 
consuming bandwidth and controller resources.

Loss of availability: 
Removing or corrupting entries can prevent devices on separate subnets from discovering or communicating with one another.
Misleading source attribution: With a Forwarded-NPDU, the outer IP source is usually the forwarding BBMD, 
while the original BACnet sender appears inside the BVLC body. Your parser should preserve both identities. 
NIST identifies BBMDs as the mechanism used to forward BACnet/IP broadcasts across IP subnets and recommends examining packet headers 
for misuse as part of firewall and intrusion-detection protections.

Foreign-device functionality introduces similar risks. 
Unauthorized Register-Foreign-Device traffic could cause a BBMD to send BACnet broadcasts to an unapproved remote system; 
Distribute-Broadcast-To-Network could then be used to inject broadcasts through the BBMD, while Delete-FDT-Entry could disconnect legitimate remote devices.
